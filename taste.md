# taste.md

Last updated: 2026-06-28

This is the living taste file for Nivedita. It should capture how she likes products, pages, and interfaces to feel and how she prefers things to be built.

Update rule: add only taste signals that are directly stated by Nivedita, visible in the repo, or clearly repeated through project decisions. Do not turn one-off experiments into permanent preferences. Put uncertain items under "To Confirm."

## Foundation Rule

- Taste is context-dependent, not fixed.
- Different products, projects, users, and problem spaces should have different visual and interaction choices.
- The right build style should follow the situation, the stakes, the workflow, and the problem being solved.
- Do not force the portfolio's current editorial taste onto every future product.
- Adapt the taste to fit the domain while keeping the underlying standards high: clarity, trust, usefulness, and strong product judgment.

## Core Taste

- Warm, strategic, human.
- Calm, thoughtful, and premium without feeling cold.
- Clear and everyday rather than overcomplicated.
- Personality-rich with restraint.
- Memorable and real rather than generic or corporate.
- "Less hype, more clarity" is a strong taste signal.
- She likes work that feels like technical range, customer lens, and shipping calm.

## How She Likes Things Built

- Start with clarity of purpose and hierarchy before decorative UI.
- Make product judgment visible, not just visual polish.
- Structure messy workflows instead of hiding complexity behind vague simplification.
- Keep systems understandable, scannable, and trustworthy.
- Show real proof, strong verbs, concrete logic, and measurable impact.
- Match the proof artifact to the product: use screens for user-facing flows and diagrams, state models, or anonymized system views for workflow-heavy systems.
- For proof surfaces, prefer custom project-specific visuals or motion over generic thumbnails when they make the product logic easier to scan.
- Use tools and AI as working methods, not as decorative badges.
- Prefer tight iteration, prototypes, and fast alignment over long abstract documentation.

## Portfolio Taste

- Current portfolio direction is light-mode, editorial, and personality-rich.
- Clay x Notion is the clearest reference point in the repo right now.
- Warm paper-like surfaces, refined serif typography, subtle hand-drawn accents, and calm motion fit her taste.
- The home page should feel lighter, more editorial, and more narrative.
- The `Work` page should be denser and optimized for recruiter scan speed.
- Shipped case studies should carry the strongest proof.
- Build experiments should act as a second proof layer showing proximity to tools, prototyping speed, and builder instincts.

## Product Surface Taste

- Enterprise and workflow-heavy surfaces should emphasize clarity, states, ownership, and reliability.
- Consumer or AI product surfaces should feel human-centered, trust-building, and easy to enter.
- When a problem is really systemic, the solution should be framed through logic, timing, states, and behavior, not just cleaner screens.
- Operational interfaces should feel calm, structured, and useful rather than flashy.
- The same person can want very different aesthetics and interaction models depending on whether the product is operational, consumer, AI-native, marketplace, internal, experimental, or portfolio-facing.

## Visual Preferences

- Strong hierarchy matters more than extra chrome.
- Typography should feel intentional and visible, not generic or faint.
- Restraint is preferred over noisy visual effects.
- Subtle motion is good when it sharpens hierarchy or presence.
- Motion should carry meaning: state change, workflow, sequence, comparison, or product behavior rather than ambient decoration alone.
- Warm neutrals and soft accents fit better than harsh corporate palettes.
- Small handmade or doodled details can work when they stay controlled.

## Writing And Content Taste

- Simple, everyday language.
- Concrete product language over vague PM buzzwords.
- Direct and factual, with minimal fluff.
- Warmth is good, but clutter is not.
- Recruiters and hiring managers should be able to scan quickly and understand context, role, signal, and where to go deeper next.

## Anti-Patterns

- Corporate-generic visuals.
- AI-template sameness.
- Bland cards and weak hierarchy.
- Overly glassy treatments.
- Overly technical aesthetics that flatten personality.
- Decorative tool badges without showing how the tools are actually used.
- Case-study storytelling that stops at surface UI instead of exposing the product logic.

## Product-Type Notes

- AI-first products: show leverage through real workflow improvement, not just novelty.
- Trust-sensitive AI products: earn belief before asking for commitment; use clear sequencing, specific explanations, and visible first-session value before adding complexity or monetization pressure.
- Financial AI products: treat explanation, trust, and monetization as one product problem rather than separate layers.
- Workflow platforms: status architecture, recovery paths, retries, and launch readiness should be treated as product decisions.
- Marketplace or systems case studies: express the solution through adaptive logic, staged experiments, and guardrail metrics.
- Safety- or attention-sensitive products: preserve the user's primary task first; use adaptive, lower-demand UI during risky moments and restore richer interaction when context is safer.
- Portfolio pages: taste and proof should work together; beautiful presentation without clear signal is not enough.
- Treat product taste as situational: choose the right level of warmth, density, polish, motion, and expressiveness based on the problem, the user's mental state, and the decision environment.
